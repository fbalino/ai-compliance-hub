## Article Body

The Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205) takes effect June 30, 2026. If your organization deploys AI systems that make consequential decisions affecting Colorado residents — in employment, credit, healthcare, housing, insurance, or education — you have 47 days to achieve compliance. Penalties reach $20,000 per violation per consumer.

Most businesses aren't ready.

### What the Colorado AI Act Requires

The Act imposes obligations on two categories of actors:

**Deployers** are organizations that use high-risk AI systems to make or substantially assist consequential decisions. For deployers, the Act requires:

1. **Impact assessments** documenting known risks of algorithmic discrimination and mitigation measures — completed before deployment and before significant changes
2. **A risk management program** including policies, governance, and human oversight of AI-assisted decisions
3. **Consumer notice** when a high-risk AI system was used in a consequential decision affecting that consumer
4. **Appeal mechanisms** giving consumers a path to challenge AI-assisted decisions

**Developers** that create and license high-risk AI systems must publish disclosures of known limitations, provide deployers with the information they need to complete impact assessments, and notify deployers of newly discovered risks.

### "Consequential Decisions" — Who Is Covered

The Act defines a consequential decision as one that has a material legal or similarly significant effect on a Colorado consumer's access to education enrollment, employment or employment opportunities, financial or lending services, essential government services, healthcare services, housing, insurance, or legal services.

If you use AI for any of the following, you are likely covered:
- Résumé screening or candidate scoring in Colorado
- Credit underwriting or loan approval for Colorado residents
- Insurance underwriting or risk scoring
- Tenant screening for Colorado rental properties
- Clinical decision support tools affecting Colorado patients
- Benefits eligibility determinations

### The June 30 Cure Period

The Colorado AG must issue a 60-day cure notice before initiating formal enforcement against a deployer that has made a good-faith compliance effort. This does not mean compliance is optional until August. It means organizations with documented, in-progress programs have a meaningful opportunity to correct specific deficiencies identified by the AG — not organizations that have done nothing.

The distinction matters: impact assessments, risk management policies, and consumer notice mechanisms must exist before the deadline, not be created in response to an enforcement inquiry.

### Five Actions to Take Now

1. **Build your AI system inventory.** List every AI system your organization uses to make or inform consequential decisions affecting Colorado residents. Include third-party and vendor-supplied AI tools.

2. **Classify each system.** Determine which systems qualify as high-risk under the Act. Document your classification rationale — the AG may request it.

3. **Complete impact assessments.** For each high-risk system, document the system's purpose, the affected population, known discrimination risks, and mitigation measures. No standard form exists — the assessment must be sufficient to demonstrate reasonable care.

4. **Implement consumer notice.** Build or configure the mechanism to notify affected consumers when AI was used in a consequential decision. Test it.

5. **Establish an appeal process.** Consumers must have a path to appeal AI-assisted decisions. Define the process, train staff, and document it.
