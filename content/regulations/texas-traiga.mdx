# Texas TRAIGA: What HR and Recruiting Teams Must Know

*Texas HB 149 | Effective January 1, 2026 | AG enforcement under DTPA*

If you use AI to screen job candidates in Texas, this law already applies to you.

Texas TRAIGA — the Responsible AI Governance Act, enacted as HB 149 — took effect January 1, 2026. It was the first major US state AI law to actually go live. Colorado's law doesn't kick in until January 1, 2027. Texas got there first. And while TRAIGA covers seven categories of AI "consequential decisions," the employment provisions are where most companies carry the most exposure and have the least documentation.

Here's what that looks like in practice: if your applicant tracking system uses AI to rank candidates, if your video interview software scores body language, or if your performance management tool generates AI-driven termination recommendations: you're operating a high-risk AI system under TRAIGA. Your obligations started January 1, 2026. If you haven't assessed your AI hiring tools yet, you're behind.

This guide focuses on what HR and recruiting teams need to know, do, and document.

---

## Why Employment AI Is TRAIGA's Biggest Compliance Target

TRAIGA covers seven domains: employment, housing, credit, education, healthcare, insurance, and legal services. Employment is first on that list for a reason.

AI in hiring has been the most regulated category of AI in the US since New York City's Local Law 144 went into effect in 2023. TRAIGA builds on that precedent and in several ways goes further. If you've addressed NYC LL 144 compliance, you have a head start. But the two programs are not the same, and you can't treat NYC compliance as a proxy for Texas compliance.

The practical reason employment AI attracts regulatory attention: it affects the most people and produces documented evidence of disparate impact at scale. An AI screening tool that processes 50,000 applications creates a data trail. When that system produces measurably different outcomes for protected groups, the AG has documented evidence for an enforcement action.

---

## What Counts as a High-Risk AI System in Employment

A high-risk AI system under TRAIGA is any AI system that makes, or is a "substantial factor" in making, a **consequential decision** about an individual.

In employment, consequential decisions include:

- Hiring and candidate screening
- Promotion and advancement
- Termination and performance evaluation
- Compensation decisions
- Work scheduling with material impact on compensation

The "substantial factor" language is where companies get caught. A system that scores candidates doesn't need to make the final decision to be covered. If a recruiter's decision is heavily influenced by an AI-generated score — even if a human technically approves it — that system is likely high-risk under TRAIGA.

TRAIGA doesn't exempt tools that are "just" recommendations. If your HR platform includes an AI hiring module you haven't thought much about, it's worth finding out whether that module qualifies. Most do.

## TRAIGA vs. NYC Local Law 144: What's Different

Both laws target employment AI. The differences are significant enough that operating in both jurisdictions means running separate compliance programs.

| Requirement | NYC LL 144 | Texas TRAIGA |
|---|---|---|
| **Geographic scope** | AI tools used for NYC employment decisions | Any high-risk AI affecting Texas employees or applicants |
| **Bias audit** | Annual third-party bias audit, results published publicly | No separate bias audit; covered by impact assessment |
| **Candidate notice** | Required before using the tool in hiring | Required when AI influences any consequential decision |
| **Opt-out mechanism** | Limited accommodation process | Explicit opt-out with an alternative pathway required |
| **Penalties** | $1,500–$500,000 per violation | Up to $10,000 per violation under DTPA |
| **Enforcement** | NYC DCWP, private right of action available | Texas AG, DTPA civil enforcement, no private right of action |
| **Annual reporting** | None | Annual report filed with Texas AG |
| **Vendor obligations** | Vendors must support the audit process | Deployer responsible; deployer must perform vendor due diligence |

The most important operational difference: NYC LL 144 requires a specific third-party bias audit with published results — a format any candidate can find and read. TRAIGA doesn't require that specific audit structure. It requires an impact assessment that documents how you tested for discriminatory outcomes, filed with the AG rather than published. The documentation obligation under TRAIGA is broader; the specific audit format under NYC LL 144 is stricter.

What TRAIGA requires that NYC LL 144 does not: a written AI risk management program, ongoing monitoring for discriminatory outcomes in production, annual AG reporting, and an explicit opt-out mechanism with an actual alternative pathway. NYC LL 144 is narrower: it targets a specific audit format. TRAIGA requires a full governance program.

If you operate in both jurisdictions, you can build toward a shared documentation foundation. But you'll need the NYC LL 144 annual audit (published externally) and TRAIGA's impact assessment (submitted to the AG) as separate deliverables.

## Your Four Core Obligations Under TRAIGA

### 1. Impact Assessment

Before deploying a high-risk AI system, you need a written impact assessment covering:

- What the system does and what it's intended to do
- Why the benefits justify the risks
- How you tested the system for discriminatory outcomes — what tests, what data, what results
- How the system handles transparency and explainability
- What human oversight exists
- How training data was collected and processed

Impact assessments aren't one-time documents. Update them annually and when you make material changes to the system or how you use it.

In practice: if you're using a vendor-supplied AI hiring tool, the vendor should provide documentation covering most of these requirements. Review that documentation critically. Don't just accept it and file it. You're the deployer under TRAIGA, not the vendor. Responsibility runs through you.

### 2. Risk Management Program

You need a written AI risk management policy. For employment AI, this means:

- Documented governance for each high-risk AI system
- A vendor due diligence process that includes requesting developer documentation
- Monitoring procedures that can detect discriminatory outcomes in production
- Training for HR staff working with AI-assisted systems

The monitoring requirement has teeth. You can't deploy a hiring AI and assume it's working correctly. You need a process — whether outcome audits, demographic analysis, or something else — that would actually catch a problem before the AG does.

### 3. Consumer Notices

When a high-risk AI system makes or substantially influences a consequential decision about a candidate or employee, you must:

- Tell them AI was used
- Explain in plain language how the AI influenced the decision
- Provide a real opt-out mechanism
- If the decision was adverse (rejection, termination, demotion), explain the basis

The opt-out requirement is more demanding than it sounds. A candidate who opts out needs an actual alternative pathway — a human-reviewed process. If your only hiring pipeline runs through an AI screening tool, you need to build around it or you can't offer a real opt-out.

### 4. Annual AG Reporting

Each year, file a report with the Texas Attorney General covering:

- All high-risk AI systems deployed in the prior year
- Impact assessments completed
- Discrimination risks identified and what you did about them
- Any known instances of algorithmic discrimination

This report goes to the AG's office, not the public — a meaningful difference from NYC LL 144's published audit requirement. But it creates a documentary record the AG can use in an enforcement action. What you self-report matters.

## Enforcement and Penalties

TRAIGA is enforced by the Texas Attorney General under the Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA) framework.

**Civil penalties:** Up to $10,000 per violation. Under DTPA, each individual decision made by a non-compliant AI system could be a separate violation. A hiring system that processed 1,000 Texas applicants without proper impact assessments or notices creates meaningful penalty exposure — do that math.

**No private right of action:** Unlike some state consumer protection laws, TRAIGA doesn't give individual consumers the right to sue. Enforcement runs through the AG's office. That reduces litigation risk but doesn't eliminate it — an AG investigation can be expensive to respond to even without penalties.

**Small business relief:** Companies with fewer than 25 employees or less than $5 million in annual revenue get scaled-down obligations, not exemptions. The AG can still pursue enforcement against small businesses for serious violations.

**Investigation triggers:** The AG is most likely to open an investigation after a complaint from an applicant, employee, or advocacy group — or after a media event around an AI hiring decision gone wrong. Companies with documented compliance programs are in substantially better position to resolve those investigations without major penalties. Companies that can't produce impact assessments or monitoring records have limited defenses.

## What You Should Do Right Now

TRAIGA is in effect. These are the immediate priorities if you haven't started:

**Inventory your AI systems.** List every AI tool used in employment decisions — your ATS screening features, video interview tools, performance management systems with AI components, and any AI that touches hiring, evaluation, or termination decisions. This inventory is the foundation.

**Request vendor documentation.** For each vendor-supplied system, ask for impact assessment documentation and bias testing results. A vendor that can't provide this creates risk for you. That's a negotiation point on your next contract renewal.

**Conduct impact assessments.** For each high-risk AI system, complete a written impact assessment. If you've already done this for Colorado AI Act compliance, review it for Texas-specific requirements and update as needed.

**Build notification workflows.** Put disclosure and opt-out mechanisms in place for candidates and employees in Texas. Check that your adverse action notices reference AI when AI was involved.

**Set up AG reporting.** Plan your annual reporting process now. You need to file with the Texas AG covering all high-risk AI systems deployed since January 1, 2026.

**Get Texas-specific legal advice.** The DTPA enforcement framework is specific to Texas and different from Colorado's approach. Engage legal counsel familiar with both the DTPA and Texas AG enforcement patterns before you finalize your compliance program.
