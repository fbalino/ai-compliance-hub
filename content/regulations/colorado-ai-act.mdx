## Overview

Colorado SB 24-205, signed May 17, 2024 by Governor Jared Polis, was the first US state law to impose impact assessment and disclosure requirements on companies that use AI to make consequential decisions affecting Colorado residents. SB 26-189, passed May 9, 2026, substantially rewrote the original text and moved the effective date to January 1, 2027.

The law's central obligation is not a compliance checklist — it is a reasonable-care standard. Under §6-1-1702, any company deploying a high-risk AI system must take reasonable care to prevent algorithmic discrimination based on protected characteristics: race, color, ethnicity, national origin, sex, religion, age, disability, and genetic information.

Two categories of companies face separate duties. Developers — companies that build AI systems for others to deploy — must supply documentation covering training data, known discrimination risks, and performance metrics across protected classes. Deployers — companies integrating those systems into their own products and services — must run impact assessments, publish consumer disclosures, and build a human appeal process before each high-risk deployment.

**The law was substantially rewritten by SB 26-189 (passed May 9, 2026). See the SB 26-189 Update section below for critical changes.**

## SB 26-189 Update (May 2026)

**SB 26-189** passed the Colorado legislature on **May 9, 2026**, substantially rewriting the Colorado AI Act. Key changes:

- **New effective date: January 1, 2027.** The original June 30, 2026 enforcement date no longer applies.
- **Mandatory AG rulemaking.** The Attorney General must conduct formal rulemaking before enforcement. This process has not yet begun as of May 2026.
- **AG guidance never published.** The February 1, 2026 deadline for AG implementing guidance was never met. SB 26-189 replaces the guidance requirement with mandatory formal rulemaking.
- **Enforcement currently stayed.** A federal magistrate issued a stay of enforcement on April 27, 2026. The stay remains in effect pending resolution.

Organizations should use the January 1, 2027 date for compliance planning, but monitor the rulemaking process and stay status for updates.

## Who It Applies To

The Colorado AI Act creates obligations for two distinct roles. The same company can be both a developer (for AI it licenses to others) and a deployer (for AI it uses internally) — and must meet both sets of requirements where that applies.

| Role | Definition | Primary Obligation |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Developer** | Creates a high-risk AI system for deployment by others | Provide documentation; disclose known discrimination risks |
| **Deployer** | Integrates a high-risk AI system into its products/services | Impact assessment; consumer disclosures; appeal process |

---

## High-Risk AI Systems

A **high-risk AI system** is any AI system that makes, or is a substantial factor in making, a **consequential decision** in one of these categories:

- **Education** — enrollment, financial aid
- **Employment** — hiring, termination, compensation, promotion
- **Financial services** — credit, lending, insurance underwriting
- **Healthcare** — diagnosis, treatment, coverage decisions
- **Housing** — rental, mortgage, real estate
- **Legal services** — access to legal representation
- **Essential government services** — benefits, licensing

### What Is NOT High-Risk?

The law explicitly excludes:
- Cybersecurity applications
- Anti-fraud systems
- AI used solely for research, testing, or development
- AI systems that only assist humans who make final decisions (with proper disclosure)

## Key Requirements

### For Deployers

Deployers have five concrete obligations under §6-1-1706 before and during deployment of any high-risk AI system.

1. **Impact Assessment (before first deployment)**

   Conduct a written impact assessment before deploying any high-risk AI system. Under §6-1-1706(1), the assessment must document:
   - The system's intended purpose and deployment context
   - Known risks of algorithmic discrimination and the protected classes at risk
   - The deployer's policies for mitigating those risks
   - Performance metrics across protected classes where available from the developer

   Assessments must be updated at least annually or whenever a material change is made to the AI system (§6-1-1706(4)).

2. **Consumer Disclosure (at decision point)**

   When a high-risk AI system makes a consequential decision, notify the affected consumer in writing:
   - That a high-risk AI system was used in the decision
   - The type and general purpose of the AI system
   - The deployer's contact information
   - How to request a human review of the decision

   The disclosure must be provided at or near the time of the decision — not buried in a privacy policy.

3. **Right to Appeal / Human Review**

   Provide consumers the ability to appeal consequential decisions made or substantially influenced by a high-risk AI system. The appeal process must result in meaningful human review — not a second pass through the same algorithm.

4. **Non-Discrimination Policy**

   Adopt a written governance policy committing to managing known algorithmic discrimination risks. This policy must be documented and retained — it forms part of the compliance record the AG can request under §6-1-1708(2).

5. **Incident Reporting (90-day window)**

   If a high-risk AI system causes or is reasonably likely to cause algorithmic discrimination, notify the Colorado Attorney General within 90 days of discovery. Notification must describe the system, the affected population, and the remediation steps taken or planned.

### For Developers

1. **Transparency Documentation**
   Provide deployers with documentation covering:
   - Intended uses and known high-risk use cases
   - Known limitations and risks of algorithmic discrimination
   - Summary of training data
   - Performance metrics across protected classes

2. **Contractual Obligations**
   Include terms in deployer contracts requiring the deployer to comply with the Act.

## Penalties & Enforcement

The Colorado Attorney General has exclusive enforcement authority. There is no private right of action.

- **Civil penalties**: Up to **$20,000 per violation**
- **Cure period**: 60-day cure opportunity before penalties attach (if the violation was not willful)
- **Injunctive relief**: AG can seek court orders to stop non-compliant practices

### The 60-Day Cure Window (§6-1-1709)

If the AG notifies you of a violation and you implement a documented cure plan within 60 days, penalties can be avoided for non-willful violations. This makes the impact assessment and governance policy doubly important: organizations with documented compliance programs have a cure plan ready before a violation is discovered — and can demonstrate good-faith remediation credibly. Organizations with no documentation have nothing to cure from.

### Current Enforcement Status

Enforcement is currently **stayed** by a federal magistrate (order issued April 27, 2026). Additionally, SB 26-189 mandates formal AG rulemaking before enforcement can begin, and that rulemaking process has not yet started. Monitor [coag.gov/ai](https://coag.gov/ai/) for updates.

## Compliance Checklist

Follow this checklist to prepare for the **January 1, 2027** effective date:

Complete these 12 steps before the Colorado AI Act enforcement date:

1. ☐ **Inventory all AI systems.** Map every AI system used in decision-making and identify which ones affect Colorado consumers.

2. ☐ **Classify each system as high-risk or not.** Does it make consequential decisions in a covered category (education, employment, financial services, healthcare, housing, legal, government)? If yes, it is high-risk.

3. ☐ **Conduct a written impact assessment** for each high-risk system. Document the system's purpose, training data, known discrimination risks, performance metrics across protected classes, and your mitigation policies.

4. ☐ **Update consumer-facing disclosures.** Add clear notices anywhere a high-risk AI system contributes to a consequential decision — including at the point of interaction.

5. ☐ **Build a consumer appeal process.** Create a mechanism for affected consumers to request human review of any adverse AI-driven decision within a reasonable timeframe.

6. ☐ **Audit vendor contracts.** If you use third-party AI tools (hiring software, credit models, clinical decision support), ensure contracts require the vendor to provide developer-level disclosures and documentation.

7. ☐ **Establish an incident reporting workflow.** If your AI causes or is likely to cause algorithmic discrimination, you must notify the Colorado AG within 90 days of discovery.

8. ☐ **Implement a written non-discrimination policy.** Adopt a governance policy committing to managing known algorithmic discrimination risks across all high-risk systems.

9. ☐ **Align your governance to NIST AI RMF.** Documenting alignment to NIST Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage functions creates a safe-harbor defense under AG guidance.

10. ☐ **Train responsible staff.** Ensure everyone who oversees or operates high-risk AI systems understands the Act's requirements and your internal compliance procedures.

11. ☐ **Schedule annual assessment renewals.** Impact assessments must be updated at least once per year or whenever a significant change is made to the system.

12. ☐ **Document everything.** Maintain a compliance file with impact assessments, governance policies, consumer disclosure templates, incident response procedures, and vendor documentation.

9. **Monitor the rulemaking.** Watch for the AG's formal rulemaking process mandated by SB 26-189. Final rules may modify specific compliance requirements.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When does the Colorado AI Act take effect?**
January 1, 2027 (under SB 26-189). The original June 30, 2026 date no longer applies.

**Does it apply to my out-of-state company?**
Yes, if you deploy high-risk AI that makes consequential decisions affecting Colorado consumers, regardless of where your company is based.

**We use a third-party AI tool for hiring. Are we responsible?**
Yes — as the deployer, you bear compliance responsibility even if you didn't build the AI. You should obtain the required developer documentation from your vendor and ensure the tool is covered by your impact assessment.

**What if our AI only flags candidates for human review — are we still in scope?**
If the AI is a "substantial factor" in the final decision, yes. Purely advisory AI with documented human override (and proper disclosure) may be treated differently, but consult legal counsel on your specific workflow.

**Has the Attorney General published implementing guidance?**
No. The February 1, 2026 guidance deadline was never met. SB 26-189 replaces the guidance requirement with mandatory formal rulemaking, but that process has not yet begun as of May 2026.
